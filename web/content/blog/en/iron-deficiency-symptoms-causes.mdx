**Iron deficiency** is the most common nutritional deficiency in the world. The World Health Organization estimates that roughly **2 billion people** — about one in four — are affected. When your blood test shows a serum iron value, that number represents the free iron circulating in your blood at that moment. But a single iron reading doesn't tell the full story. Ferritin, transferrin saturation, TIBC, and hemoglobin all contribute to a complete picture of your iron status. This guide explains what iron does in your body, what the numbers on your blood test mean, what causes iron to drop or rise, and when to act on your results.

## What Does Iron Do in Your Body?

**Iron** is a mineral your body needs to produce hemoglobin — the protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen from your lungs to every tissue. About **70% of your body's iron** is bound to hemoglobin and myoglobin (a muscle protein), while the remaining **30%** is stored as ferritin and hemosiderin in your liver, spleen, and bone marrow.

Iron's role extends well beyond oxygen transport. It is essential for **energy production**, **immune function**, and **brain development**. According to the National Institutes of Health (NIH), iron serves as a cofactor for more than 300 enzymes. When iron runs low, all of these processes slow down — which is why the symptoms range from fatigue and brain fog to weakened immunity.

Your body cannot manufacture iron on its own. All iron comes from food or supplements. **Heme iron** from animal sources (red meat, fish, poultry) is absorbed at roughly **15–35%** efficiency, while **non-heme iron** from plant sources (legumes, spinach, dried fruits) is absorbed at only **2–20%**. This absorption gap is why vegetarians and vegans face a higher risk of deficiency.

## Normal Iron Levels: Reference Ranges

Evaluating iron status requires more than a single number. Doctors typically order a panel of tests. Based on Mayo Clinic and clinical guidelines, the widely accepted reference ranges are:

| Parameter | Men | Women |
|-----------|-----|-------|
| **Serum iron** | 65–175 µg/dL | 50–170 µg/dL |
| **Ferritin** | 24–336 ng/mL | 11–307 ng/mL |
| **Transferrin saturation** | 20–50% | 15–50% |
| **TIBC** | 250–370 µg/dL | 250–370 µg/dL |

Reading these together tells a clearer story:

- **Low serum iron + Low ferritin + High TIBC**: Classic iron deficiency
- **Low serum iron + High ferritin + Low TIBC**: Anemia of chronic disease (the body has iron stored but cannot use it due to inflammation)
- **Ferritin < 15 ng/mL**: Definite iron deficiency per WHO criteria
- **Transferrin saturation < 16%**: Strongly suggests iron insufficiency

Lab reference ranges vary. One result in isolation is far less useful than a **trend over time**. A ferritin of 25 ng/mL means something very different if it was 60 three months ago versus stable at 25 for the past year. ## What Causes Iron Deficiency?

Iron deficiency rarely has a single cause. Multiple factors often overlap.

### Inadequate Dietary Intake

**Poor dietary iron intake** is the most common cause worldwide. The recommended daily allowance is **18 mg for women**, **8 mg for men**, and **27 mg during pregnancy** (NIH). Diets low in red meat, legumes, and dark leafy greens fall short of these targets. Vegetarians and vegans are at particular risk because plant-based non-heme iron is absorbed at roughly one-third the rate of animal-based heme iron — meaning they may need to consume **1.8 times** the standard recommended intake (Institute of Medicine).

### Blood Loss

**Blood loss** is the leading cause of iron deficiency in premenopausal women. Heavy menstrual periods (menorrhagia) can account for a monthly loss of **12–15 mg of iron**. In men and postmenopausal women, gastrointestinal bleeding from ulcers, polyps, or hemorrhoids is the first cause to investigate. Regular blood donors lose approximately **250 mg of iron** per donation; those donating 3–4 times per year can see ferritin levels drop significantly.

### Malabsorption

Even with adequate dietary intake, the gut may fail to absorb iron efficiently. **Celiac disease** is a notable example — studies have found iron deficiency anemia in **46% of newly diagnosed celiac patients** (Harper et al., 2007). Inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn's, ulcerative colitis), gastric bypass surgery, and medications that reduce stomach acid (proton pump inhibitors, antacids) all impair iron absorption. Stomach acid is essential for converting dietary iron into a soluble form the intestine can absorb.

### Increased Physiological Demand

Certain life stages demand more iron than usual. **Pregnancy** increases blood volume by 50% and requires extra iron for fetal development — daily needs jump from 18 mg to **27 mg**. **Breastfeeding** transfers about 0.3 mg of iron to the infant daily through breast milk. **Adolescent growth spurts** increase iron needs, and when combined with the onset of menstruation in teenage girls, the risk of deficiency rises sharply.

## Iron Deficiency Symptoms

Symptoms develop gradually, and many people attribute them to stress or poor sleep before considering iron. The spectrum runs from mild depletion (low ferritin, normal hemoglobin) to full iron deficiency anemia (low hemoglobin).

### General Symptoms

**Persistent fatigue** is the hallmark symptom. When iron is insufficient, hemoglobin production drops, tissues receive less oxygen, and the body cannot produce energy efficiently. A clinical study found that **72% of women** with ferritin below 30 ng/mL reported unexplained fatigue — and when ferritin was raised above 50 ng/mL through supplementation, fatigue scores improved significantly (Vaucher et al., 2012, CMAJ).

**Dizziness** and **headaches**, especially upon standing, are common. **Shortness of breath** and **heart palpitations** appear first during exertion and may extend to rest as anemia deepens. The body compensates for reduced oxygen-carrying capacity by increasing heart rate — producing the sensation of a racing or pounding heart.

### Physical Signs

Skin **pallor** is one of the most visible signs — as hemoglobin decreases, skin, nail beds, and the inner surface of the lower eyelids lose their color. **Hair thinning** and **brittle nails** are early warnings that iron stores are running low. In advanced cases, nails may develop a concave, spoon-like shape known as **koilonychia**.

**Cracks at the corners of the mouth** (angular cheilitis), a **sore or swollen tongue** (glossitis), and **difficulty swallowing** are less recognized but specific indicators of iron deficiency. **Restless leg syndrome** — an uncomfortable urge to move the legs, particularly at night — occurs in **25–35%** of iron-deficient patients (Allen et al., 2013).

### Iron Deficiency in Children

In children, iron deficiency directly affects cognitive development. WHO data shows that anemia prevalence among preschool-aged children is **42.6% globally**. **Attention problems**, **learning difficulties**, and **behavioral changes** are frequently observed in iron-deficient children. Pica — craving and eating non-food substances like ice or dirt — is a notable sign of iron deficiency in pediatric populations.

## Diagnosing Iron Deficiency: Which Tests Are Ordered?

When iron deficiency is suspected, doctors typically begin with a **complete blood count** (CBC) to check hemoglobin and hematocrit levels. An **iron panel** follows: serum iron, ferritin, TIBC, and transferrin saturation.

**Ferritin** is considered the single most reliable marker of iron stores. The American Academy of Family Physicians (AAFP) reports that ferritin, when ordered alongside a CBC, detects iron deficiency anemia with **95% accuracy** (Guyatt et al., 1992). However, because ferritin is an acute-phase reactant, it can appear normal or elevated during infection or inflammation even when iron stores are low — checking **CRP** alongside ferritin helps clarify the picture.

## Treatment Approaches

Treatment depends on the cause and severity. The first step is always to **identify and address the underlying cause** — prescribing iron supplements without investigating the reason for depletion misses the point.

**Oral iron supplements** are the most common intervention. Iron sulfate, iron fumarate, and iron gluconate are widely used forms. Standard dosing is 100–200 mg of elemental iron daily, but recent research shows that **alternate-day dosing** (every 48 hours) may actually improve absorption by reducing hepcidin elevation (Stoffel et al., 2017, The Lancet Haematology). Taking iron supplements with **vitamin C** increases absorption by **2–3 times**; tea, coffee, and dairy should be avoided at the same time, as they inhibit absorption.

**Intravenous (IV) iron** is used when oral supplements are not tolerated, absorption is impaired, or rapid correction is needed. A single IV iron infusion can significantly raise ferritin levels within weeks.

Treatment typically lasts **3–6 months** — iron stores need time to refill even after hemoglobin returns to normal. Monitoring ferritin throughout treatment confirms whether stores are actually replenishing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can iron deficiency cause hair loss?

Yes. Iron deficiency is one of the most common yet underdiagnosed causes of hair loss, particularly in women. Hair follicles are among the fastest-dividing cells in the body and require adequate iron and oxygen to sustain growth. Dermatology studies have found that women with **ferritin below 30 ng/mL** have a significantly higher rate of telogen effluvium — a form of diffuse hair shedding (Park et al., 2013). Some specialists recommend an optimal ferritin level of **50–70 ng/mL** for hair health specifically. Once iron stores are replenished through supplementation, hair shedding typically slows over **3–6 months**, though regrowth can take longer.

### What is the difference between iron deficiency and iron deficiency anemia?

They are two different stages of the same condition. **Iron deficiency** means your body's iron stores are depleted — ferritin drops, but hemoglobin may still be normal. **Iron deficiency anemia** is the later stage where stores are so depleted that hemoglobin production falls below normal levels. The WHO defines anemia as hemoglobin below **12 g/dL in women** and **13 g/dL in men**. Catching iron deficiency before it progresses to anemia matters because early-stage depletion is easier and faster to correct. Ferritin is the best tool for early detection — it drops before hemoglobin does.

### How long should I take iron supplements?

Oral iron supplementation typically continues for **3–6 months**. Hemoglobin usually starts rising within the first 2–4 weeks, but filling iron stores takes longer. Guidelines recommend continuing treatment for at least **3 months after hemoglobin normalizes** to ensure stores are adequately replenished (WHO guidelines). A ferritin level above **50 ng/mL** is generally considered a reliable sign that stores are sufficiently restored. Regular blood tests during treatment are important both to track progress and to prevent iron overload, which carries its own risks.

### Which foods help or hurt iron absorption?

**Vitamin C** is the strongest dietary enhancer of iron absorption. A glass of orange juice (approximately 75 mg of vitamin C) consumed with an iron-rich meal can increase non-heme iron absorption by **2–3 times**. The **MFP factor** found in meat, fish, and poultry also enhances non-heme iron uptake. On the other hand, **tannins and polyphenols** in tea and coffee can reduce absorption by **50–60%** — iron supplements or iron-rich meals should not be consumed alongside these beverages. **Calcium** from dairy products and **phytates** from whole grains and legumes also inhibit absorption when consumed in the same meal.

### When and how should I get my iron tested?

An iron panel is best drawn **in the morning after fasting** for at least 8 hours. Serum iron levels fluctuate by up to **30% throughout the day**, with morning values being the highest and most consistent. If you take iron supplements, stop them **24–48 hours before the test** for accurate results. When comparing results over time, getting tested under the same conditions (fasting vs. fed, morning vs. evening) ensures consistency. A full iron panel — serum iron, ferritin, TIBC, and transferrin saturation — provides a far more complete picture than serum iron alone.
