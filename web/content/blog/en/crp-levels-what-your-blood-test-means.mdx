**CRP (C-reactive protein)** is one of the most commonly ordered blood tests for detecting inflammation in your body. Produced by the liver, CRP surges when infection, injury, or chronic disease triggers an immune response — sometimes rising to **1,000 times its baseline** within 24–48 hours. The American Heart Association (AHA) recognizes high-sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP) as a standard tool for cardiovascular risk assessment, with levels above **3 mg/L** signaling elevated heart disease risk. Yet many people see "CRP" on their blood test results and have no idea what the number means. This guide covers what CRP measures, what the ranges mean, what causes abnormal results, and how tracking CRP over time gives you a clearer picture than any single reading.

## What Is CRP?

**C-reactive protein (CRP)** is an acute-phase protein produced by the liver in response to inflammation. When tissue damage, infection, or chronic disease activates your immune system, cells release interleukin-6 (IL-6), which signals the liver to ramp up CRP production. This makes CRP a reliable **early-warning marker** — it detects inflammation before many other signs appear.

In healthy adults, CRP typically sits below **0.3 mg/dL (3 mg/L)** (NCBI, 2025). When inflammation starts, CRP begins rising within **6–8 hours** and peaks around **48 hours**. Once the inflammatory trigger resolves, CRP falls quickly — its half-life is approximately **19 hours**. That rapid rise-and-fall makes CRP especially useful for monitoring treatment response: if antibiotics are working, CRP should drop within days.

CRP does not tell you *where* the inflammation is or *what* is causing it. It tells you that something inflammatory is happening in the body. Doctors combine CRP with other tests to locate the source.

## Normal CRP Ranges

CRP is measured using two types of tests: standard CRP and high-sensitivity CRP (**hs-CRP**). Both measure the same protein, but hs-CRP detects much lower concentrations and is used specifically for cardiovascular risk stratification.

### Standard CRP Reference Values

| CRP Level | Interpretation |
|-----------|---------------|
| **< 0.3 mg/dL (< 3 mg/L)** | Normal — seen in most healthy adults |
| **0.3–1.0 mg/dL (3–10 mg/L)** | Mild elevation — minor infection, obesity, or smoking |
| **1.0–10.0 mg/dL (10–100 mg/L)** | Moderate elevation — active infection or inflammatory disease |
| **> 10.0 mg/dL (> 100 mg/L)** | Marked elevation — severe bacterial infection or sepsis |

### hs-CRP and Cardiovascular Risk

According to the AHA and CDC classification:

| hs-CRP Level | Cardiovascular Risk |
|-------------|---------------------|
| **< 1.0 mg/L** | Low risk |
| **1.0–3.0 mg/L** | Moderate risk |
| **> 3.0 mg/L** | High risk |

The JUPITER trial (2008) studied **17,802 participants** with hs-CRP above 2.0 mg/L and normal LDL cholesterol. Statin therapy reduced heart attack and stroke risk by **44%** in this group — demonstrating that hs-CRP identifies cardiovascular risk even when cholesterol looks fine.

Labs may report CRP in mg/dL or mg/L (a tenfold difference). Always check which unit your lab uses. A single elevated reading is less meaningful than a **trend across multiple tests** — even a common upper respiratory infection can temporarily spike CRP for several days.

## High CRP: Causes and Symptoms

Elevated CRP means inflammation is present somewhere in your body. The degree of elevation provides clues about severity.

### Common Causes of High CRP

**Infections** are the most frequent driver of CRP elevation. Bacterial infections typically push CRP above **50 mg/L** — a meta-analysis found that CRP > 50 mg/L indicated bacterial infection in approximately **90% of cases** (Simon et al., 2004, Clinical Chemistry). Viral infections usually produce a milder response, with CRP staying in the 10–40 mg/L range. This difference helps clinicians distinguish bacterial from viral illness.

**Chronic inflammatory diseases** cause sustained CRP elevation. Rheumatoid arthritis, lupus (SLE), inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn's, ulcerative colitis), and **vasculitis** all raise CRP during active disease flares. In rheumatoid arthritis, CRP is a standard metric for tracking disease activity alongside joint symptom scores.

**Cardiovascular disease** has a direct relationship with CRP. Chronic low-grade inflammation in arterial walls drives atherosclerosis progression. The Framingham Heart Study showed that individuals with hs-CRP above 3 mg/L had a **2–3 fold increase** in heart attack risk. After an acute myocardial infarction, CRP can exceed **200 mg/L**.

**Cancer**, particularly advanced solid tumors, can elevate CRP through tumor-derived cytokines like IL-6. Additionally, **obesity** raises CRP through chronic low-level inflammation generated by adipose tissue. Adults with a BMI above 30 have mean CRP levels roughly **80% higher** than normal-weight individuals.

### Symptoms

High CRP itself does not produce symptoms — it reflects what's happening underneath. The symptoms depend on what's driving the inflammation:

- **With infection**: fever, chills, fatigue, localized pain and swelling
- **With autoimmune disease**: joint pain, morning stiffness, skin rashes, chronic fatigue
- **With cardiovascular risk**: often no symptoms at all — which is why hs-CRP screening matters

Persistently elevated CRP without an obvious cause may signal silent chronic inflammation. Your doctor will investigate with additional tests (ESR, procalcitonin, blood cultures, autoimmune panels) to find the source.

## Low CRP: Is It a Concern?

Low CRP is generally good news — it indicates no significant inflammation in your body. Levels below **0.3 mg/dL** are expected in healthy individuals. However, there are rare situations where a misleadingly low CRP warrants caution:

- **Immunosuppressive therapy** (post-transplant, chemotherapy) — a suppressed immune system may not produce CRP even when infection is present
- **Severe liver failure** — since the liver produces CRP, severe hepatic damage can impair production

Outside these exceptions, a low CRP requires no further investigation and is a reassuring finding.

## When Should You Get a CRP Test?

Doctors most commonly order CRP when they suspect **infection**. Fever, fatigue, and localized pain combined with an elevated CRP confirm active inflammation and help gauge its severity. During antibiotic treatment, tracking CRP decline is one of the most practical ways to confirm **whether treatment is working** — a CRP that stays elevated despite antibiotics suggests the therapy needs adjustment.

**Autoimmune disease monitoring** relies heavily on regular CRP measurements. In rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, and inflammatory bowel disease, CRP helps detect flares early. The European Alliance of Associations for Rheumatology (EULAR) guidelines include CRP as a standard parameter in rheumatoid arthritis disease activity scores.

**Cardiovascular risk screening** with hs-CRP adds an extra layer of information, especially for people in the intermediate risk category. The AHA recommends hs-CRP testing when traditional risk factors (cholesterol, blood pressure, smoking) leave the risk category unclear. Post-surgery, CRP is also commonly tracked to watch for complications.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What diseases cause high CRP levels?

High CRP is not specific to any single disease — it signals **inflammation from any source**. The most common causes are bacterial infections (where CRP often exceeds 50 mg/L), autoimmune conditions (rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, Crohn's disease), cardiovascular events (CRP can surpass 200 mg/L after a heart attack), and certain cancers. Even obesity raises CRP mildly through chronic low-grade inflammation from adipose tissue. The key point is that elevated CRP is a warning flag, not a diagnosis. Your doctor will use additional tests to identify the specific cause.

### What CRP level is considered dangerous?

CRP above **10 mg/L (1.0 mg/dL)** is clinically significant and points to active inflammation. Levels above 50 mg/L typically indicate serious infection, while values exceeding **100 mg/L** suggest severe bacterial infection or sepsis requiring immediate medical attention. For cardiovascular risk, an hs-CRP above **3 mg/L** places you in the high-risk category according to AHA guidelines. However, a single high reading is not cause for panic — even a temporary flu can spike CRP for several days. Persistent elevation or a rising trend over multiple tests warrants thorough medical evaluation.

### What is the difference between CRP and ESR (sed rate)?

Both measure inflammation but respond at different speeds. CRP rises within **6–8 hours** of inflammation onset and drops quickly once the cause resolves — its half-life is about 19 hours. **ESR (erythrocyte sedimentation rate)** is slower: it takes days to rise and weeks to normalize. This makes CRP the more responsive indicator for acute situations and treatment monitoring. Doctors often order both together — CRP captures the current state while ESR reflects the inflammation profile over a longer period. In practice, a falling CRP with a still-elevated ESR usually means the acute problem is resolving.

### How can you lower CRP levels?

The most effective way to lower CRP is to **treat the underlying inflammation** — antibiotics for infection, immunosuppressive therapy for autoimmune disease, and so on. Beyond treating the root cause, lifestyle changes reduce chronic low-grade inflammation: at least **150 minutes per week** of moderate exercise (WHO recommendation), a Mediterranean-style diet rich in omega-3 fatty acids, weight loss (bringing BMI into the normal range can reduce CRP by approximately **30%**), and quitting smoking. Statins also lower hs-CRP through anti-inflammatory effects — the JUPITER trial showed statin use reduced hs-CRP by **37%** independent of cholesterol lowering.

### Do I need to fast before a CRP blood test?

Fasting is not required for a CRP test — CRP levels are not directly affected by recent meals. However, since CRP is often ordered alongside tests that do require fasting (complete blood count, lipid panel, glucose), most labs prefer a **morning blood draw after 8–12 hours of fasting** for consistency. When comparing CRP results over time, testing under similar conditions — same time of day, same fasting state — helps ensure accurate trend tracking. A series of consistent measurements always provides more reliable information than any single result.
